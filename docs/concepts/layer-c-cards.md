# Layer C — Cards

Layer C writes the prose a reader actually sees: a short, **fully cited** summary card
for each shelf and theme. Where Layers A and B are structure, Layer C is the
human-facing explanation — and it is held to a strict grounding rule.

## Every claim cites a chunk

A card is generated by an LLM, but it is **not** free-form. The model is given the
shelf's or theme's member chunks (a theme card sees the theme's chunks; a shelf card sees
the shelf's) and must ground every statement in them, tagging each with the chunk ids it
relied on. Nothing is meant to come from the model's *parametric memory* — what the LLM
"already knows" from pretraining.

This is enforced by `config.layer_c.grounding_check`: **`strict`** (default) rejects or
flags any sentence whose cited chunks don't support it; `lenient` warns; `off` disables
the check. So a claim the model wanted to write but couldn't cite — say, a health benefit
not present in any member chunk — is dropped rather than shipped.

Here is a populated card (the `mammalian milk product` shelf):

> **Calcium in dairy milk** — *evidence_quality: high*
> Milk supplies ~300 mg of calcium per cup `[tb_0421]`, and its lactose content aids
> calcium absorption `[tb_0421, ab_088]`. Fortified milk also provides vitamin D
> `[tb_0421]`.
> *tip:* a cup of milk covers roughly a quarter of an adult's daily calcium.
> *cited_chunk_ids:* `[tb_0421, ab_088, gd_0203]`

Every clause carries a citation; the `cited_chunk_ids` list is the card's audit trail back
to the corpus. The full pipeline behind this card is in the
[worked example](worked-example.md).

```python
class Card(BaseModel):
    card_id: CardId
    target_id: str                  # the shelf_id or theme_id this card describes
    target_type: Literal["shelf", "theme"]
    title: str
    summary: str
    tip: str | None
    evidence_quality: EvidenceQuality      # high | medium | low | debated | unclear
    controversy_note: str | None           # when sources disagree
    confidence_note: str | None
    cited_chunk_ids: list[str]              # provenance for every claim
    llm_model: str
    prompt_version: str
    safety_flagged: bool                    # gate for unsafe/medical-overreach output
    generated_at: datetime
```

## Honest about evidence

Nutrition literature disagrees with itself constantly, so a card carries metadata that
keeps it honest rather than authoritative:

- **`evidence_quality`** grades the *overall* strength of the chunks behind the card:
  `high` → `medium` → `low`, plus `debated` (sources genuinely conflict) and `unclear`
  (too thin to judge).
- **`controversy_note`** is the *specifics* of any disagreement — the prose that explains
  what conflicts and why. So `evidence_quality="debated"` is the flag; `controversy_note`
  is the explanation that accompanies it.
- **`safety_flagged`** marks a card that strays into unsafe or medical-overreach territory
  (e.g. dosing advice, disease-treatment claims). It's set by the grounding/safety pass,
  weighted toward `config.layer_c.safety_sensitive_facets` (e.g. `allergies`), so such
  cards can be withheld or reviewed.

Together these let a downstream answer say *"the evidence is debated, and here are the
sources on both sides"* instead of inventing a confident consensus.

## Where cards plug in

Cards hang off the graph next to what they describe, and the retrieval path can fold a
card's `evidence_quality` into ranking:

```python
fs.build_layer_c()
fs.graph.shelf("foodon:olive_oil").card().summary
fs.graph.theme("...").card().cited_chunks()      # back to the source chunks
```

Because the card cites chunks and the chunks carry full provenance (source doc,
section, FoodOn IDs), the trail **card → chunk → source** is auditable end to end.
